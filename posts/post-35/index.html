Spotify Removed 75 Million AI Spam Tracks. Seventy-Five. Million.

February 25, 2026 • 3 min read • Category: AI economics

Spotify quietly revealed it removed 75 million spam tracks in a single year — a number so large it almost rivals its legitimate 100 million-song catalogue. That’s not a typo. That’s an alternate artificial music universe. The scheme was beautifully cynical: AI tools could mass-produce ambient instrumentals, meditation loops, ultra-short tracks, and deepfake celebrity songs faster than moderation systems could blink. And because Spotify pays royalties on streams longer than 30 seconds, scammers simply needed volume — oceans of algorithmically generated background noise — to siphon off real money.

The result was what you might call the perfect spam economy. Algorithms generate music. Algorithms recommend music. Algorithms collect payments. Meanwhile, human artists watch their royalties get diluted by thousands of “Calm Study Rain Forest Alpha Frequency (432Hz)” uploads created in bulk. Some efforts were almost impressive in their audacity — including deepfake tracks like Heart on My Sleeve, which mimicked Drake and The Weeknd convincingly enough to rack up streams before being pulled. At one point, nearly a third of daily uploads on competing platforms were reportedly fully AI-generated. Not curated. Generated.

Spotify now says it’s deploying AI to detect AI spam — a recursive arms race where machines police other machines while musicians hope to survive the crossfire. The company insists this hasn’t meaningfully impacted payouts. But when 75 million fake tracks slip through the gate before being cleared out, it raises an uncomfortable question: are we building streaming platforms optimized for art — or for upload velocity?
