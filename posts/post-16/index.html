AI invents a hot spring that doesn’t exist.

January 28, 2026 • 3 min read • Category: travel tech

Earlier this year, Tasmania Tours published a blog highlighting a serene destination called the Weldborough Hot Springs. According to the post, the springs offered a peaceful forest escape, mineral-rich pools, and a favorite stop for hikers exploring northern Tasmania. It looked like exactly the kind of hidden gem travelers hope to stumble across — remote, tranquil, and just obscure enough to feel special.

Then tourists started showing up. Visitors arrived in the small town of Weldborough asking locals for directions to the springs. They called the local hotel. They stopped at the pub. And each time, they were met with the same response: no one had ever heard of them. The confusion grew as more people arrived, convinced they were simply missing a turnoff or a trailhead.

The reveal came later. The hot springs weren’t hard to find — they didn’t exist at all. The blog post had been generated using AI after Tasmania Tours outsourced its marketing content to a third party. While posts are normally reviewed before publication, this one went live while the owner was out of the country. Somewhere in that process, an AI filled in the blanks with a destination that sounded plausible enough to pass a quick read, complete with confident descriptions and convincing imagery.

The company removed the article and apologized, but the episode stuck because it perfectly illustrates a new kind of modern mistake. Nothing was hacked, exaggerated, or intentionally misleading. An AI-generated article simply described a place that felt real enough, and humans trusted it. In this case, the result wasn’t dangerous — just confusing and a little funny. But it’s a reminder that when AI is used to generate content at scale, it doesn’t just summarize reality. Sometimes, it quietly invents it.
