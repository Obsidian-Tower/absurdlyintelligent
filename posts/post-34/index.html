AI mistook Doritos for a gun.

February 24, 2026 • 2 min read • Category: surveillance tech

Somewhere in Maryland, a teenager finished a bag of Doritos, crumpled it up, and put it in his pocket like a normal person. Unfortunately, a school weapon-detection system allegedly looked at that pocket and said: “GUN.” The result was the least snack-friendly escalation imaginable: multiple police officers showing up and handcuffing a 16-year-old because Cool Ranch apparently has “tactical vibes.” :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

The most absurd part is how perfectly this captures the modern AI experience: it’s not predicting the future, it’s confidently identifying crunchy shapes. Real weapon detection in messy, real lighting is hard, and false positives aren’t just “oops” moments, they’re “guns pointed at a kid” moments. And once the system yells “threat,” humans tend to respond like it’s a fire alarm, not a maybe-alarm. Turns out the gap between “computer vision” and “common sense” is approximately the size of one empty chip bag. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

This is why these deployments deserve skepticism: they don’t have to be wrong often to be a problem, they only have to be wrong at the worst possible time. Today it’s Doritos. Tomorrow it’s a phone, a wallet, a hairbrush, a clarinet case, a hoodie pocket doing hoodie pocket things. If we’re going to put AI in charge of yelling “WEAPON,” the bar shouldn’t be “usually fine,” it should be “almost never humiliates and endangers someone over snacks.” :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}
