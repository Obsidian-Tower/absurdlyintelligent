Taco Bell’s AI drive-thru has opinions.

January 22, 2026 • 2 min read • Category: fast food automation

We have automated the ordering of tacos. Not the cooking. Not the delivery. The part where a human says “hi, what can I get you?” Taco Bell rolled out AI-powered voice ordering in hundreds of U.S. drive-thrus, and in theory it’s there to make everything faster, smoother, and more accurate. In practice, it’s like pulling up to a talking menu that has read every training manual on customer service and now wants to run your life. You say “one thing,” it says “wonderful choice,” and suddenly you’re negotiating with a robot about beverages you never asked for.

To be fair, the system has processed a ton of orders successfully. The problem is that the internet doesn’t care about the two million normal orders. The internet cares about the moments where the AI accepts an order for 18,000 cups of water, or gets stuck in a loop like it’s trying to upsell you into bankruptcy. And the funniest part is that none of this is “evil AI,” it’s just the usual modern reality: the software is doing its best, and humans are immediately stress-testing it for sport. We can’t even be trusted with a touchscreen soda machine. Now we have a conversational one.

Taco Bell’s apparent takeaway is what every company eventually learns: full automation sounds cool until lunch rush shows up. The new vibe seems to be a hybrid approach where humans step in when things get busy, which is corporate speak for “AI is great until it meets real life.” And honestly, that’s the whole story of automation right now. We keep trying to remove humans from simple interactions, then act surprised when the results feel slightly weird. Not “the future,” exactly. More like “a beta test you experience while hungry.”
